Stanford Visit #2

Marty recently went on his second trip to Stanford Cancer Center for a biopsy and follow up visit with his oncologist, Dr. Fan. The CT-guided biopsy was performed on the right flank mass and was done to determine if the mass is still renal cell carcinoma or if the cells had mutated into a different type of cancer (over a period of time cancer cells can change and mutate into different strands). Dr. Fan believes the mass is still kidney cancer but wanted to do this biopsy for research and as a type of "insurance". Depending on the results, they can discover the best way to go about treating the lesion. We are currently waiting for the final report.

Marty saw Dr. Fan and her medical team following the biopsy. We updated her on the completion of his radiation treatment on June 10th. Marty had 10 rounds of radiation to four sites in his body - two in the pelvis, one in the brain and one in the spine. A MRI scan is planned for the beginning of August to determine if the radiation worked. Dr. Fan will review the scans to see if the radiation stabilized the brain lesion. If so it will open the door up to clinical trials that involvement treatment. On a side note- Marty has had the best results with radiation so our fingers are crossed!

In the meantime, Marty is going to start a new oral chemo drug called Votrient for lesions in the lungs, liver and flank. Votrient treats late stage kidney cancer by cutting off the blood supply to the lesion. If the lesion doesn't have blood & oxygen supply via the blood vessels then the tumor will shrink and die. Dr. Fan had suggested Marty take part in an observational clinical trial that would scan the blood vessels attached to the right flank lesion through a high-tech CT scanner that has been recently engineered. Marty would be the third person at Stanford to do this clinical trial. Dr. Fan's hypothesis is that you can tell if a cancer treatment is working or not by looking at the blood vessel growth surrounding the targeted lesion. If the blood vessels have shrinked then the cancer treatment is doing it's job. If the blood vessles haven't shrunk then the treatment isn't working. The CT scan can show changes in the blood vessels in as little as 7-8 days following the first day of treatment. So for future cancer treatments the time it could take to see if a drug is working can be reduced from months to days. Overall it was a good trip down to Stanford. Marty stayed in Half Moon Bay at a family friend's house and was able to relax and enjoy the ocean views. He will return to Stanford twice in the next month for the 2 CT scans (one scan before he starts Votrient and one scan 8-15 days after he starts the drug). We will keep updates posted!
